# MVP Project Reflection

This MVP project was genuinely one of the most enjoyable things I've built so far and honestly, the end result surprised even me. What started as a simple black background with bold orange fonts evolved into something that looked like a highly polished, professional Hot Rod forum page. It turned out to be one of my finest personal designs to date.

## Decisions

One of the bigger decisions I made since P01 was to include a photo viewer grid gallery on my Gallery page. I ran into a limitation early on in Canva, while it's a great platform overall, it doesn't offer a built-in photo gallery feature in its web design tool. I had to go searching. After some research and asking Claude for suggestions, I was pointed toward [Elfsight.com](http://Elfsight.com) and [Squarespace.com](http://Squarespace.com). Elfsight stood out right away for its ease and simplicity. I was able to build the gallery directly in their platform, grab the embedded link, and drop it into my Canva site using the embed app tool. Once I found the right tool, the process was smooth. I actually found two photo viewer styles I liked, so I went ahead and included both.

## What Worked

The features I'm most proud of are the reviews and show submission forms on my Shows and Reviews page. Beyond just fulfilling the assignment criteria, both forms serve a real purpose by allowing visitors a way to contribute to the page, whether that's submitting a show they want to promote or sharing a review of one they attended. The show submission form felt like a no-brainer for building community exposure. The review form took a little more thought and work but then it clicked that it wasn't like an API pushing content live. It's a form I can review and approve before anything goes public, which is a great built-in filter against anything inappropriate or off-brand. I created both forms using an interactive tool from [JotForm.com](http://jotform.com). The platform made it easy to build each form directly on their site, then export an embedded link and plug it into my Canva page using the embed app tool.


## What I'd Do Differently

If I could change one thing, it would be my choice of design platform. I went with Canva expecting it to have everything I needed, and while it's a solid tool, I ended up having to look elsewhere to fill in the gaps. That wasn't something I anticipated. That said, it was a worthwhile experience, I walked away more familiar with Canva and with a better sense of how to problem-solve when a tool doesn't do exactly what you need it to.

## What You Learned

The biggest takeaway from this whole process was how much you learn through iteration. Testing your design with real people, users who have no idea what your intentions were is invaluable. Peer feedback has a way of revealing blind spots you'd never catch on your own. Sometimes what makes perfect sense to the creator doesn't land the same way for the user, and having someone point that out is exactly the kind of feedback that makes the final product better. I measured my success by how naturally visitors moved through the page and by how few questions I got about where things were or what they did. The fact that there weren't many questions told me the design did its job.

I learned how to research and identify third party tools such as [JotForm.com](http://jotform.com) and [Elfsight.com](http://elfsight.com) to create the interactive features my site needed. These tools allowed me to build a better user experience by giving visitors a way to contribute content directly to the site, whether that was submitting a show or leaving a review of one they had previously attended. That firsthand perspective from the community gives future visitors something valuable to read and reference when deciding whether a show is worth the trip.

Perhaps the most unexpected connection was to project management and time management.
Those courses put organizational discipline into perspective in a way I did not fully appreciate
until I was managing a real build with real deadlines. Having a well thought out plan, putting
objectives on paper, and setting milestones was not just an academic exercise. It directly
influenced how I structured my time and ultimately allowed me to deliver a polished, complete
product rather than a rushed one. Without that foundation, the design and build would not have
come together as cleanly or as intentionally as they did.
